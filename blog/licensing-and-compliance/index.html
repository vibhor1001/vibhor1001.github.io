Licensing & compliance for UK short-term lets

PropertyFlow·Updated 2026·6 min read

Short-term let regulation has changed more in the last four years than in the previous twenty. What you need depends heavily on where the property is, so it's worth taking each nation in turn.

Scotland: licensing is live

Scotland has run a mandatory short-term let licensing scheme since October 2022. Every host needs a licence from their local authority, with conditions covering safety, insurance and maximum occupancy. Letting without one is a criminal offence, with fines of up to £2,500. Edinburgh and the Highlands have been especially active on enforcement.

England: a register is coming

Under the Levelling-up and Regeneration Act 2023, England is introducing a mandatory national registration scheme, with a target of 2026 (though the date has slipped before). Once live, every let will need a registration number displayed on listings, and platforms will be expected to check it. A new "C5" planning use class for short-term lets has also been proposed. In Greater London, a separate rule already caps whole-property short lets at 90 nights a year without planning permission.

Wales & Northern Ireland

Wales is rolling out a visitor-accommodation register in 2026, the first stage of reform that also brings a visitor levy and, later, statutory licensing. Northern Ireland has required Tourism NI certification for years, the longest-standing regime of the four.

The safety duties that apply everywhere

Whichever nation you're in, a common set of safety obligations applies: a fire risk assessment, smoke and carbon-monoxide alarms (interlinked in Scotland), an annual Gas Safety Certificate where gas is present, an electrical installation report (EICR) every five years, and a valid EPC where one is required in England and Wales. These are due regardless of any licensing or registration scheme.

This is a general summary, not legal advice, and rules change often. Always check your local authority's current requirements.
